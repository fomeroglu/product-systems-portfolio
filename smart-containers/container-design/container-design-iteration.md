# Container design iteration

**Product:** Reusable trackable metal container  
**Design arc:** Controlled field pilot → validated improvements → scaled production  
**Outcome:** 1,000+ units deployed of final version

---

## Container overview

The container was designed as a modular physical and digital interface between different package handling systems — sortation equipment, trailers, forklifts, robots, and dock employees. The standard pallet-based frame allowed compatibility across environments without requiring infrastructure changes at any facility.

**Physical compatibility built into the base spec:**
- Compatible with automated sortation equipment
- Compatible with pallet dumper operations
- Net sides allow dock employee access for loading and unloading
- Forkable from any side — compatible with forklifts, pallet jacks, and robots
- Higher load factor than standard pallets and gaylords due to structural netting design

---

## Design iteration arc

The product went through a deliberate staged approach — starting with a controlled field pilot to gather real operational data before committing to full-scale production. Each stage informed the next.

### Stage 1 — Controlled field pilot

An initial batch of containers was deployed to early adopter customers in real operational environments. The goal was not to achieve full-scale deployment but to observe how the container performed under actual logistics conditions before locking in the production design.

**What the pilot revealed:**
Field operation exposed design refinement opportunities across structural components, hardware, and net attachment — insights that would not have been visible in a lab or controlled test environment. The mechanical demands of real logistics operations — repeated forklift handling, long-haul transit vibration, loading and unloading cycles — provided data that directly shaped the next iteration.

**Why this approach was right:**
Starting with a pilot rather than going straight to full production is the correct call for a physical product with this many operational variables. The cost of learning early is far lower than the cost of discovering design gaps at scale.

---

### Stage 2 — Design validation and refinement

Based on pilot learnings, targeted improvements were made across the container design. A structured refurbishment and validation program was run before redeployment — each improved unit went through a test sequence covering structural integrity, sortation compatibility, and movement handling.

**Key improvements from this stage:**
- Net attachment system reinforced for sustained load performance
- Hardware upgraded for vibration resistance under transit conditions
- Validation protocol established and documented for future batches

The refurbishment program doubled as a real-world design test — every change was validated under live conditions before being carried into production.

---

### Stage 3 — Scaled production batch

With the design validated, production was scaled to a larger batch incorporating all learnings from stages 1 and 2.

**Key improvements:**
- Frame material upgraded for significantly stronger structural performance
- Net design updated to address refinement opportunities identified in the pilot
- All hardware improvements from stage 2 built in from the start
- Updated branding and labeling

---

### Stage 4 — Full redesign for scale

A comprehensive redesign informed by two complete production cycles of field data. Structural improvements were made across all major components — frame, panels, hardware, and attachment points. This was the version that went to 1,000+ unit deployment.

A key design decision in this batch involved evaluating two back panel mounting approaches — one prioritizing structural strength with load distributed across the frame, the other prioritizing assembly simplicity. The stronger option was selected.

Physical design changes in this batch also incorporated learnings from the tracker pilot — tracker placement and orientation guidelines were built into the design to optimize signal performance without requiring post-deployment adjustments.

---

## Design-to-telemetry feedback loop

One of the more distinctive aspects of this product was the feedback loop between physical design and digital telemetry. Tracker data directly informed physical decisions:

- **Signal performance** — telemetry analysis revealed GPS signal was affected by tracker mounting position relative to the metal structure. Led to updated placement guidelines and a change in default tracker configuration.
- **Orientation sensitivity** — cellular antenna performance was affected by tracker orientation. Added to installation documentation as a critical step.
- **Maintenance access** — battery replacement access designed into the container so maintenance could be performed without full disassembly in the field.

This feedback loop — field data informing physical design, physical design improving field data quality — was a meaningful differentiator from traditional container products that treat the physical and digital layers as separate concerns.
